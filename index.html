Chapter One ·· Origins & Naming

Why Magdalene? Why Laundries?

✦

The name carries the entire weight of a theological judgement. Mary Magdalene was not, according to modern biblical scholarship, a prostitute. The gospels describe her as a woman from whom Jesus cast out seven demons, and as the first witness to the Resurrection — which in early Christianity would have made her the first apostle. She is, in the original texts, a figure of witness and courage, not of sexual sin. For centuries, the Catholic Church suppressed this reading entirely.

In 591 AD, Pope Gregory I formally merged three separate biblical women into one composite figure of sexual sin and penitence: Mary Magdalene, Mary of Bethany (who anointed Jesus's feet with oil), and the unnamed "sinful woman" of Luke 7:36–50. This conflation was not supported by scripture. It was theologically constructed — and it was useful. The woman who lamented her sins and was redeemed became the patron saint of a system designed to contain, punish and erase women deemed morally impure.

The Feminist Theological Reclamation

In 1988, Pope John Paul II formally acknowledged that Mary Magdalene had been incorrectly identified as a sinner. In 2016, Pope Francis elevated her feast day to the rank of an Apostle — the Church acknowledging, four centuries later, that Gregory I had been wrong. But the damage was catastrophic and long-lasting. The "fallen woman" category the error produced confined and destroyed hundreds of thousands of lives across the Western world. That a misreading of three gospel women underpinned a global carceral system for two centuries is not a footnote — it is the institutional history of female sexuality under Catholic authority.

Why Laundries?

The choice of laundry work was both symbolic and profitable. Washing soiled linen was a literalisation of spiritual cleansing — the "fallen" women would scrub the dirt from the world's clothes as they, theoretically, scrubbed the sin from their souls. It was also a commercial enterprise. The laundries took in washing from hotels, hospitals, universities, military barracks, schools, and government institutions. The women laboured without wages, making the convents financially self-sustaining. Hot steam-filled rooms, industrial mangles, vats of boiling water, heavy irons — this was not incidental hardship. It was the architecture of a system designed to produce obedience and obliterate identity.

Global Origins, Irish Specificity

Magdalene asylums were not an Irish invention. The first recorded institution appeared in Hamburg in 1618. England established one in 1758, and the very first in Ireland — a Protestant institution on Lower Leeson Street in Dublin — opened in 1765, founded by Lady Arabella Denny. The first Catholic-run asylum in Ireland opened in 1798. What made Ireland distinctive was how long the institutions survived, how deeply they became embedded in the state, and how thoroughly they were insulated from scrutiny. While other countries largely wound down their Magdalene institutions by the mid-20th century, Ireland's last laundry did not close until 1996.

To understand why, it helps to trace the global arc of the institution — where it came from, how it evolved across different countries, and why the Irish version departed so radically from what existed elsewhere. The same basic idea, born in Enlightenment England and spread across the English-speaking world — to Scotland, Wales, the United States, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and Sweden — produced vastly different outcomes depending on the political and religious conditions in which it took root. Nowhere did it survive as long, or become as punitive, as in Ireland.

The Founding Moment — London, 1758

The modern Magdalene institution was born not in a convent but in Enlightenment England. The Magdalen Hospital for the Reception of Penitent Prostitutes opened in Whitechapel, London, in 1758 — founded by the philanthropist Jonas Hanway, the silk merchant Robert Dingley, and the magistrate John Fielding. It was a Protestant institution. Crucially, it was conceived as a short-term rehabilitation centre: women could leave, were paid a small sum, and were intended to re-enter society. That transformation — from refuge to prison — came later, and nowhere more completely than in Ireland. By 1900, over 300 Magdalene-type institutions existed in England alone.

England

300+ institutions by 1900. Progressive inspection and labour regulation softened conditions. Mostly closed or converted by the 1950s. Last British institutions wound down by the early 1960s.

Scotland

Edinburgh Royal Magdalene Asylum (1797); Glasgow's Magdalene Institute. Scholar Jowita Thor (2019) finds Scottish institutions were regulated and significantly transformed. Edinburgh's last closed 1958.

United States

Magdalen Society of Philadelphia, founded 1800 by Bishop William White. Spread to New York, Boston, Chicago — run by Protestants, Catholics, and the Salvation Army. Subject to public oversight and the emerging social work profession.

A Dissenting Voice — Charles Dickens & Urania Cottage

In 1846, Charles Dickens and philanthropist Angela Burdett-Coutts founded Urania Cottage in Shepherd's Bush, London — explicitly because Dickens believed the existing Magdalen Hospitals were too harsh. He called it simply "a Home for Homeless Women." Women were given domestic skills, education, and passage to Australia where they could start afresh. The contrast with the Irish Catholic model — indefinite, punitive, unpaid, silencing — is precise.

The Scottish Comparison — Why It Matters

Scotland is the most instructive comparison for Ireland. Both were strongly religious societies with deeply embedded institutional traditions. Both had Magdalene institutions from the late 18th century. Yet the trajectories diverged dramatically. Scottish institutions were inspected, regulated, and progressively integrated into secular social welfare frameworks. Women could, for the most part, leave. Scotland's last Magdalene institution closed in 1958 — thirty-eight years before Ireland's. The key differentiating factor: the relationship with the state.

Five Reasons Ireland Lasted Longest

1. The Church-State Alliance of 1922. While England and Scotland progressively subjected charitable institutions to state inspection and labour regulation, the Irish Free State moved in the opposite direction — formally delegating authority to the Catholic Church and exempting convent laundries from all oversight.

2. Self-financing through unpaid labour. As Mary Raftery documented, the laundries "had little impact on prostitution over the period, and yet they were continuing to multiply and expand due to their self-supporting free labour." There was no financial incentive to close.

3. Post-colonial identity. The new Irish state defined itself against Protestant liberal Britain by being emphatically more Catholic, more morally rigorous, more "pure." Historian Rebecca Lea McCarthy argues that "the process of colonization, and of defining a national image, determined the nature and longevity of the Magdalene Laundries" in Ireland specifically.

4. No welfare state alternative. When Britain built the National Health Service (1948) and expanded its welfare state, it created secular alternatives to Church-run institutions — and with them, leverage to close those violating minimum standards. Ireland had no equivalent.

5. The distinctively Irish character of the institution. As James Smith observes, the Irish variety shifted from short-term rehabilitation to indefinite, involuntary detention — women had no legal right to leave, no mechanism to appeal, no access to the outside world. This was not a refuge. It was a prison operating entirely outside the criminal justice system.

The Vocabulary of Containment

Language was the first instrument of the system. The specific words used in admission records, court orders, and Church documents were not bureaucratic accidents — each one was doing ideological work. Together they created a social reality in which the confinement of women appeared natural, even compassionate.

"Fallen woman"

The foundational category. A woman who had transgressed sexually — or was suspected of having done so, or might do so — was "fallen." Once fallen, she required "rescue." The word naturalised judgement by making it sound like a physical state.

"Penitent"

Theological framing of punishment as spiritual cleansing. The women were not prisoners — they were penitents performing penance. Unpaid labour was "therapeutic rather than gainful occupation," as one Reverend Mother described it. The word absolved the institution of any commercial dimension.

"Occasion of sin"

A specific Catholic theological concept: a person, situation, or object that leads another person to sin. Applied to women's bodies, it made women themselves the source of moral danger — not the men who might be "tempted." The inversion made containment of women a moral necessity.

"Moral danger"

A legal category in Irish law that could trigger state-sanctioned removal of a person. A girl deemed to be "in moral danger" could be committed by a court without criminal conviction. It required no evidence of wrongdoing — only the likelihood of future transgression, as defined by the committing authority.

"Training school"

The euphemism used most commonly in New Ross and elsewhere for the convent laundry. "The training school girls, as they were known" — this is how Keegan describes the community's language in the novel. The word "training" implied rehabilitation and education; the reality was forced unpaid labour.

"Rescue" / "Reform"

The language used by religious orders for what was, in many cases, abduction or coerced commitment. "Rescue missions" retrieved women from streets or poorhouses. The orders presented themselves as saviours — a framing that made any resistance by the women appear as ingratitude or spiritual deficiency.

Chapter Three ·· The State & The Church

How Independence Became a Theocracy

✦

When Ireland won its independence in 1922, the new Free State faced a defining question: what would it be? The answer, shaped by nationalist politicians deeply intertwined with the Catholic hierarchy, was resounding — it would be everything Britain was not. It would be Gaelic, it would be Catholic, and it would be morally pure. This ideological programme had catastrophic consequences for women.

The Education Paradox — and the Irish Language

The new state's priorities are revealed by what it chose to build first. The revolutionary government established a Ministry for the National Language, responsible for education in Irish-speaking Gaeltacht districts. The 1922 National Programme Conference made Irish compulsory in all national schools within months of independence — infants were to be taught entirely through Irish.

De Valera's Cabinet and the Missing Education Minister

The Church's grip on schooling was so total that, when Éamon de Valera formed his first government in 1932, he did not even appoint a Minister for Education. There was no need — the Catholic Church managed all primary schools and the vast majority of secondary schools. The state paid over 95% of costs but exercised almost no oversight. The Church's position was explicit and accepted without challenge: education of Catholic children must be "in Catholic schools, by Catholic teachers, under Catholic control."

The Nuns — A Paradox of Power

The four religious orders that operated the Magdalene laundries occupied a uniquely contradictory position in Irish society. They were among the only educated, professionally autonomous women in Ireland. By entering religious life, they had escaped the very domestic imprisonment that Article 41.2 prescribed for all other women — they were not required to be wives, mothers, or domestic servants. They had careers, authority, institutional power, and public respect. And then they enforced that same imprisonment on others.

This paradox is not incidental. The nuns who ran the laundries had themselves submitted to a parallel form of institutional control — the rigid hierarchy of their orders, the vow of obedience, the suppression of individual will in service of the institution. Some historians argue that many nuns were themselves "true believers" who genuinely thought they were saving souls. Others were, in varying degrees, complicit out of obedience rather than conviction — following orders within a system they had not designed and could not challenge. The system produced nuns who were also, in some ways, its instruments rather than its architects. This complicates but does not diminish accountability.

What is documented is the institutional behaviour: the renaming of women, the physical punishments, the commercial exploitation of unpaid labour, and the refusal, to this day, to open archives or contribute to survivor compensation. Whatever the internal spiritual lives of individual nuns, the collective institutional record is unambiguous.

Sisters of Our Lady of Charity

Operated Drumcondra and Seán McDermott Street, Dublin. Their 1992 land sale triggered the discovery of the mass grave. Their High Park convent held 155 bodies, only 75 with death certificates.

Good Shepherd Sisters

Operated Cork, Limerick, Waterford, and New Ross — the convent that inspired Keegan's novel. Motto: "I will seek the one that is lost." When survivor Rose applied for her records, they sent back one ledger line: "Penitent — twice." Her two children were listed as offences.

Sisters of Mercy

Operated Galway and Dún Laoghaire. Also ran many of Ireland's industrial schools. The 1937 Conditions of Employment Act specifically exempted their laundry operations from all labour regulations.

Religious Sisters of Charity

Operated Donnybrook and Cork. Classified as charitable works; operated as commercial enterprises with unpaid labour. State-funded for decades without inspection.

Chapter Five ·· Inside the Laundries

Life Behind Convent Walls

✦

Once inside, women entered a world designed to strip them of identity and will. The regime was organised around three pillars: silence, labour, and penance.

The Stripping of Identity

Civilian clothes were removed and replaced with a drab uniform. Names were taken and replaced with saints' names or numbers — in the novel, the girl tells Furlong she "goes by Enda in here," a boy's name assigned to erase her gender identity as well as her personal one. Women were forbidden to discuss their origins or families. Contact with the outside world — letters, visits, telephone calls — was restricted or denied entirely.

The Work and Punishments

Women stood for long hours over industrial washing machines, mangles, and ironing boards in steam-filled rooms. They handled enormous quantities of linen from hotels, hospitals, restaurants, and military barracks. Punishments for infractions — talking, showing emotion, attempting to escape — included verbal humiliation, physical beatings, denial of food, isolation in rooms known as "the box," and having one's hair forcibly cut.

Food as a Mechanism of Control

Oral histories reveal food was a constant instrument of power. Meals were monotonous and often inadequate. Former survivor Bridget cannot do laundry today without the steam triggering a return to the washroom. The sensory prison of the laundries followed women out of the walls for the rest of their lives. In 2011, the UN Committee Against Torture stated that girls in the laundries were "deprived of their identity, of education and often of food and essential medicines and were imposed with an obligation of silence and prohibited from having any contact with the outside world."

The Commercial Dimension — Who Benefited?

Despite their status as registered charities — which meant full tax exemption and state funding — the laundries operated as commercial businesses. Their clients included: government departments, the Irish Army, hospitals, hotels, restaurants, universities, schools, and private households. Ireland was, in the most literal sense, sending its dirty laundry to be washed by women it was imprisoning.

Known clients of Magdalene laundries

  • Government hospitals and the health system
  • Hotels, guesthouses, and the hospitality trade

What happened to the money

The profits generated by the women's unpaid labour were reinvested in the religious orders' property portfolios, overseas missions, and institutional buildings. When the McAleese Report was published in 2013, the orders had accumulated substantial assets. The compensation scheme established by the Irish government was funded entirely by the state — the orders contributed nothing. They have never accounted for the revenue generated by the women's labour.

The irony of the Christmas card. In Keegan's novel, when the Mother Superior gives Bill Furlong an envelope containing £50 for Christmas, the card inside depicts The Flight into Egypt — Mary and Joseph fleeing with the infant Jesus to escape persecution. The nuns who are imprisoning a woman and her baby send a Christmas card depicting a mother and child fleeing to refuge. The theological irony is total. The card is signed "For Eileen, Bill and Daughters" — folding the Furlong family into complicity, making them named recipients of the institution's goodwill.
Chapter Six ·· Silence, Society & Complicity

Did Ireland Know? The Architecture of Silence

✦

This is the question that haunts Irish collective memory. The answer, uncomfortable but inescapable, is: yes — and no. The laundries were not secret. They were present in the landscape. Their linen was in every hotel. Their existence was discussed in whispers. What the society maintained was not ignorance but something more subtle: a collective will not to know, not to speak, and most certainly not to protest.

The Open Secret

James Smith, in his landmark study Ireland's Magdalen Laundries and the Nation's Architecture of Containment (2007), coined this phrase deliberately. In Keegan's novel, the town's ambivalence is documented with precision: reports circulate about the convent — some true, some not — and the community reaches for uncertainty as a way of not knowing. "People said lots of things — and a good half of what was said could not be believed; never was there any shortage of idle minds or gossips about town." This cultivated doubt is the mechanism of collective silence. Keegan shows it operating in real time.

Why Didn't People Speak Out?

  • The Church's moral authority was essentially total. To question the nuns was to question God — in a society where the Church ran hospitals, schools, and social services, this was not a theoretical risk.
  • Shame was weaponised. Families who had sent women to the laundries had a direct interest in keeping the secret. The same shame used to commit women kept them silent upon release.
  • Survivors were isolated. Women released were often given no notice, no money, no support. Many moved to England immediately and never spoke of what had happened — even to their own children.
  • Ostracism was real. Anyone who raised questions about the nuns risked being seen as immoral, antinational, and ungodly. Mrs Kehoe's warning to Furlong in the novel is the community's explicit articulation of this cost: "these nuns have a finger in every pie."
  • There were no legal mechanisms. Without a legal process for commitment, there was no legal process for challenge.

The Diaspora — Women Who Left and Never Spoke

Many women released from the laundries went straight to England on release and never spoke of it again — not to friends, not to partners, not to their own children. The Irish emigrant community in Britain was large enough to absorb them without questions. They rebuilt their lives in Liverpool, London, Birmingham. They married, worked, raised children. And they carried the walls with them.

When the RTÉ documentaries aired in the 1990s, some of those women's children — now adults in England — were discovering for the first time why their mother would sometimes go very quiet, or why she never talked about her past in Ireland. The silence crossed the Irish Sea and crossed generations. Joe Costello, a former Labour Party politician who lived near a Magdalene Laundry as a young man, described the broader dynamic: "It was a cover-up by all levels of society and people wouldn't accept, even as late as the early 90s. Fully grown adults, political people simply rejected the obvious."

Ireland was a nation immersed in a conspiracy of silence since the last decades of the twentieth century concerning institutional abuse in which the clergy was involved.

— James Smith, Ireland's Architecture of Containment, 2007

The 1980s — Cracks in the Dam

Unemployment reached 17% in the 1980s. Over 200,000 emigrated between 1983 and 1990. Yet the same crisis was fracturing the social consensus. Ann Lovett's death (1984) — a 15-year-old who died alone giving birth at a grotto in Granard — forced a terrified public to confront what happened to girls who became pregnant outside marriage. The Kerry Babies case exposed grotesque Garda treatment of a single mother. Individual tragedies were the cracks in the dam. The dam broke in the 1990s.

Chapter Seven ·· The Celtic Tiger & The Reckoning

How Everything Came to Light

✦

What Was the Celtic Tiger?

The term — first used in a 1994 Morgan Stanley report by Kevin Gardiner — described Ireland's extraordinary economic transformation from the mid-1990s to the late 2000s. Drawing on low corporate tax rates, EU Structural Funds, and foreign direct investment, Ireland went from one of Western Europe's poorest countries to one of its wealthiest. GDP growth of 7–10% per year. A country that had bled emigrants for two centuries began attracting immigrants. The social consequences were as dramatic as the economic ones: secularisation accelerated, and for the first time, significant numbers of Irish people were willing to challenge the Church's authority. The economic boom produced a cultural confidence — a willingness to critique the past — that had been impossible in a poorer, more frightened society.

The Ferns Report — Where Keegan's Novel Begins

In 2005, the Ferns Report was published — an investigation into decades of clerical sexual abuse in the Diocese of Ferns. The Diocese of Ferns covers County Wexford, including the town of New Ross. It is the same diocese, the same county, and the same institutional landscape as Keegan's novel. Within the broader investigative context that the Ferns Report opened up, accounts emerged of Church institutional abuse in Wexford — including accounts related to the Good Shepherd Sisters' convent in New Ross. Keegan has cited this investigative context as central to her writing of the story. The novel is, among other things, a fictional response to documented real events in a specific place she knows.

1992–93: The Breaking Point

The immediate trigger was a real-estate transaction. When the Sisters of Our Lady of Charity sold land at their High Park convent, they applied to exhume 133 bodies. The exhumation found 155 bodies. Only 75 had death certificates. The nuns cremated all remains immediately and reburied them. When journalists realised that the identities of nearly half the dead women were unknown to the state — erased by the nuns — the story could no longer be contained.

The Documentaries

  • 1996 — "Sex in a Cold Climate" (Channel 4): The first documentary to feature Magdalene survivors' direct testimonies. Ireland's sexual repression put on trial.
  • 1999 — "States of Fear" (RTÉ/Mary Raftery): The most comprehensive reckoning. Taoiseach Ahern wept on camera and issued an apology to industrial school survivors.
  • 2002 — "The Magdalene Sisters" (Peter Mullan): Won the Golden Lion at Venice. The Church called it "a grotesque distortion." Survivors called it accurate.
  • 2024 — "The Woman in the Wall" (BBC): A drama series exploring the long-term psychological consequences of the laundries on survivors and their children, set in the present day.

Sinéad O'Connor — Witness and Dissenter

One figure connects the institutional history and the cultural reckoning with unusual precision. Sinéad O'Connor was sent to An Grianán Training Centre in Dublin at the age of 14 — a Magdalene-affiliated institution whose name used the same "training" euphemism deployed in New Ross and elsewhere across Ireland. She was labelled a "problem child." She described the institution as a prison where girls were abused, cut off from their families, and forced to work without pay. In October 1992 — the same month the Donnybrook mass grave story broke — she tore up a photograph of Pope John Paul II on live American television. The act was widely condemned at the time as incomprehensible or attention-seeking. In retrospect it was one of the most precisely targeted protests in modern Irish cultural history. She knew exactly what she was doing and why. It would take the rest of the country another decade to understand it.

The McAleese Report (2013) & State Apology

After years of lobbying by Justice for Magdalenes and a 2011 UN recommendation, the Irish government commissioned an Inter-Departmental Committee. Its central finding: the Irish state was significantly and directly involved in the admission of women to Magdalene laundries — through courts, gardaí, health boards, and state-funded institutions. Over 11,000 women documented since 1922. Taoiseach Kenny delivered a formal apology. The religious orders did not. They have contributed nothing to the compensation scheme.

The Spanish Parallel

Ireland was not alone in building a Catholic-nationalist carceral system for women. In Spain, under Francisco Franco's dictatorship, similar institutions called the Patronato de Protección a la Mujer (Board for the Protection of Women) operated from the 1940s. Girls and women who did not conform to society's moral rules were confined and "re-educated" in institutions closely modelled on the same ideology. These institutions remained active until 1985 — the exact year of Keegan's novel. The Spanish novelist Almudena Grandes wrote about the Patronato in her Civil War saga, notably in Las tres bodas de Manolita. The parallel demonstrates that the Magdalene system was not a specifically Irish aberration but a product of Catholic-nationalist state formation wherever it took hold.

Chapter Eight ·· Women's Rights in Ireland

Then and Now — A Century of Law and Struggle

✦

The Magdalene laundries did not exist outside the law — they were, in a precise sense, a product of it. The legal framework built in the first decades of Irish independence systematically excluded women from public life, criminalised their reproductive autonomy, and inscribed their domestic role into the Constitution itself. Understanding that framework — and tracing its slow, contested dismantling — is essential to understanding both the laundries and why they lasted as long as they did.

The Regressive Framework — Building the Cage

1922
Women over 21 get the vote in the Free StateOne of the few genuinely progressive acts of the early state — extending equal suffrage without the age restriction still applied to British women. However, this formal equality masked deep institutional discrimination.
1925
Juries Act — women effectively excluded from jury serviceWomen were entitled to opt out of jury duty, and most were expected to. In practice, juries were almost exclusively male for decades.
1932
The Marriage Bar introducedWomen in the civil service were required to resign upon marriage. Women's economic independence was structurally eliminated. The Marriage Bar remained in force until 1973.
1935
Contraception bannedThe Criminal Law Amendment Act prohibited the importation, sale, and advertisement of contraceptives. Ireland would not fully liberalise access to contraception until 1985.
1937
Bunreacht na hÉireann — Article 41.2The Constitution defines a woman's "life within the home" as essential to the common good. Divorce is prohibited. The document was written in close collaboration with Archbishop McQuaid. It remained substantially unchanged for 80 years.

The Long Dismantling — Decade by Decade

1970
Commission on the Status of Women establishedThe first formal state recognition that women's equality was a public policy matter, not merely a private one.
1971
The "Contraceptive Train"Women's liberation activists travel to Belfast to buy contraceptives, then return to Dublin and openly declare them to customs officers in open defiance of the law. A defining act of feminist civil disobedience.
1973
Marriage Bar abolished — EEC membership obliges itIreland's accession to the European Economic Community brought an obligation to comply with equal treatment directives. The Marriage Bar, which had stood for 41 years, was eliminated not by domestic political will but by European law.
1977
Employment Equality ActProhibits discrimination on grounds of sex or marital status in employment. A landmark piece of legislation, though enforcement remained limited in practice.
1979
Family Planning Act — contraceptives on prescriptionThe first partial liberalisation. Contraceptives became legally available in Ireland for the first time — but only on prescription and only for "bona fide family planning purposes." Full availability without prescription came only in 1985.
1983
The Eighth Amendment insertedA constitutional ban on abortion is added by referendum (67% in favour). Ireland would carry this ban for 35 years — during which women who needed abortions were forced to travel to England at their own expense.
1990
Mary Robinson elected first female PresidentA landmark symbolic and cultural moment. Robinson transforms the presidency into an active voice for human rights, emigrant communities, and marginalised groups.
1992
The X CaseA 14-year-old rape victim is initially prevented by court injunction from travelling to England for an abortion. Public fury forces multiple referendums on the right to travel and to information.
1995
Divorce legalised — barelyA referendum to legalise divorce passes by the narrowest possible margin: 50.3% in favour. The closeness of the result is a precise measure of how contested social change remained.
2015
Marriage Equality by referendumIreland becomes the first country in the world to legalise same-sex marriage by popular vote — 62% in favour. A country that criminalised homosexuality in 1993 votes for equality 22 years later.
2018
The Eighth Amendment repealed — 66% in favourIreland votes overwhelmingly to remove the constitutional abortion ban. The repeal is a direct reversal of the theocratic project of 1937 — women's bodily autonomy finally protected in the Constitution that had denied it for 81 years.

2024: The "Woman in the Home" Clause — Still There

On International Women's Day, 8 March 2024, Ireland held a referendum specifically to remove the Article 41.2 "woman in the home" language from the Constitution — the very clause that had underpinned the ideology behind the Magdalene laundries. The government recommended a "yes" vote. The referendum failed — approximately 68% voted against the proposed replacement wording. The reasons were contested: the new wording was widely criticised as vague and poorly drafted, and many voters were uncertain what they were actually being asked to change. But the outcome means that the language defining women's role as primarily domestic — written by De Valera in close consultation with Archbishop McQuaid in 1937 — remains in the Irish Constitution in 2025. The longest sentence in that story is not yet finished.

Women in Ireland Today

Ireland today is a dramatically changed society. The country has had two female presidents (Mary Robinson, 1990–97; Mary McAleese, 1997–2011) and a female Tánaiste (deputy prime minister). Gender quotas introduced in 2012 have increased the proportion of women TDs (Members of Parliament) from under 15% to approximately 25% — still low by European standards, but a significant change. The gender pay gap stands at approximately 11%. Women make up the majority of university graduates. Legal protections against discrimination are extensive.

Yet the structures that enabled the laundries — the delegation of care work to under-resourced institutions, the underfunding of social services, the gap between legal equality and lived experience — have not been fully dismantled. The religious orders that operated the laundries still control significant assets, still refuse to open their archives, and have contributed nothing to the compensation of the women they imprisoned.

Joni Mitchell's "The Magdalene Laundries" (1994)

In 1994 — one year after the Donnybrook mass grave discovery shocked Ireland — the Canadian singer-songwriter Joni Mitchell released "The Magdalene Laundries" on her album Turbulent Indigo. Mitchell, whose mother was of Irish-Scottish descent, wrote the song in direct response to news of the graves. It is one of the first works of popular culture to address the laundries internationally, and its release preceded Peter Mullan's film by eight years.

The connection is personal as well as political. Mitchell had given up a child for adoption in 1965 at age 22 — a circumstance she described as being "completely broke in a strange city, Toronto, where I knew no one." She later said she wrote the song partly in recognition that her own circumstances could have led her to a similar institution. The song's perspective is interior and specific — a woman inside the laundry, speaking.

A Note on Forced Adoptions

The Magdalene laundries were connected to the forced adoption of infants through the mother-and-baby homes system. Women who gave birth in these institutions routinely had their children taken for adoption — often to families in the United States and Australia — without meaningful consent. Ireland's Adoption (Information and Tracing) Act (2022) finally gave adult adoptees the legal right to access their birth records. Many survivors have spent decades searching for children taken from them inside these institutions. Some have reunited. Many have not. The children taken are a separate but inseparable part of this history.

The Hierarchy of Victimhood

Scholars have documented a troubling disparity: industrial school victims received a survivor-led, nine-year inquiry that ran to thousands of pages and is widely accepted as credible. Magdalene survivors received an 18-month administrative inquiry that excluded their testimonies from its findings, minimised the abuse, and was described by scholars as a "document of limited truth." The difference may reflect the demographics: industrial school victims were children; Magdalene survivors were adult women whose moral status had been permanently tainted by the very system that abused them. Even in seeking justice, they were judged.

Chapter Ten ·· Literature
Reading the History Through Literature

Small Things Like These

Claire Keegan — 2021 · New Ross, County Wexford, 1985

Claire Keegan's novella is one of the finest pieces of literary fiction to emerge from the Magdalene experience. At just over 100 pages, it achieves what volumes of reportage sometimes cannot: the rendering of moral complicity as a felt experience, something that happens in the body as much as in the mind. Reading it alongside the historical record illuminates both.

The Epigraph & The Dedicatory

Before the narrative begins, two paratextual elements establish the frame that everything else sits inside. The dedicatory reads: "This story is dedicated to the women and children who suffered time in Ireland's mother and baby homes and Magdalen laundries. And for Mary McCay, teacher." The collective comes first — the anonymous plural of historical victims. Then the personal: a named teacher, identified only by what she did. Teaching in Keegan's moral universe is the act of making someone believe they matter as much as any other person. It is what Mrs Wilson does for Bill. It is what the laundries systematically destroyed.

The epigraph is the 1916 Proclamation of the Irish Republic: "The Irish Republic... guarantees religious and civil liberty, equal rights and equal opportunities to all its citizens... cherishing all of the children of the nation equally." By placing the founding promise of the Irish state on the first page of a novel about the Magdalene laundries, Keegan performs an act of radical, controlled accusation. She doesn't editorialize. She just puts the promise there — and then tells the story of what the state did instead. The gap between the two is the entire novel.

Setting & Historical Accuracy

The novel is set in New Ross, County Wexford, in 1985 — the location of a real Magdalene laundry operated by the Good Shepherd Sisters, in the same diocese (Ferns) investigated in the 2005 Ferns Report, in the same year the Spanish Patronato de Protección a la Mujer closed, and in the depths of Ireland's worst economic crisis since the Famine. Bill Furlong is a coal and timber merchant. On Christmas Eve, delivering coal to the Good Shepherd convent, he discovers a young girl locked in the fuel shed, barefoot, terrified, clearly imprisoned.

◆ Bill Furlong — Character Construction

Bill was born on April Fool's Day, 1946, to an unmarried 16-year-old domestic worker. "Some said the boy would turn out to be a fool." He is marked from birth. His mother's employer — Mrs Wilson, a Protestant widow — kept her on rather than turning her out. Bill spent his infancy in a Moses basket in Mrs Wilson's kitchen, on a floor he later recognised resembled a draughts board: pieces that jump over others, or are taken. He jumped. The women in the laundry were taken. Mrs Wilson taught him to read, gave him A Christmas Carol, made him use a dictionary, and told him he was "a credit to yourself" — for a whole day, he went around believing he mattered as much as any other child. That belief is what he eventually extends to Sarah.

◆ Mrs Wilson, Ned & Daily Grace

Ned, the farmhand who lived at Wilson's, is likely Bill's biological father — a discovery Bill approaches obliquely in the barber's mirror, seeing a resemblance he "both could and could not see." If true, Ned spent a lifetime performing unannounced, unnamed love: polishing Bill's shoes, buying his first razor, teaching him to shave, staying up talking at the fire, weeping after Bill's mother died. "An act of daily grace," Bill thinks, to make someone believe they came from finer stock. The title's meaning extends to Ned as well as Mrs Wilson: small things, added up, amounting to a life. The novel traces where that inheritance leads when it is finally tested.

◆ Eileen — The Cost of Knowing

Eileen is not a villain. She is the novel's most precise portrait of complicity — not through wickedness but through fear and social conformity. "If you want to get on in life, there's things you have to ignore, so you can keep on." When Bill invokes Mrs Wilson's example, Eileen responds with a class argument: Mrs Wilson could afford to be generous because she was wealthy. She had land, pension, social insulation. She wasn't risking anything. Eileen is partially right — and Keegan doesn't let her be simply wrong. The novel holds both: Bill's moral clarity and Eileen's realistic assessment of the cost. Their daughters' education depends on the nuns. Their livelihood depends on community standing. The system sustains itself through fear that is not unreasonable.

◆ The Mother Superior's Tea

The most sophisticated portrait of institutional menace in the book. She makes tea, cuts cake, uses the diminutive "Billy." She asks about his daughters, notes they're in her school and choir, says: "Won't they all soon find themselves next door, in time to come, God willing." She is threatening him over tea. She probes for weakness: "But it must be disappointing, all the same... To have no boy to carry on the name." And Furlong's response — "What have I against girls? My own mother was a girl, once" — is quiet and devastating. He refuses every trap. Then she hands him the envelope.

◆ Sunday as Moral Structure

Sunday runs through the novel as a recurring failure of peace. Furlong cannot perform the Irish Sunday — Mass, pub, newspaper, fire — because his conscience won't allow it. "Sundays could feel very threadbare, and raw." He wants it to be Monday, so work can drown out what he knows. Sunday is the day that strips away the protective cover of routine and forces conscience to the surface. It is also, liturgically, the day of Resurrection — of moral awakening. The novel's action takes place across two Sundays and a Christmas Eve. The structure is the argument: what will you do when you cannot hide in work?

◆ Why Christmas?

Christmas is the feast of the Incarnation — the moment God enters the world of the vulnerable, the cold, the dispossessed. The nativity story is itself the story of an unmarried woman who is pregnant — exactly the category of woman the laundries imprisoned. The women in the coal shed are the anti-nativity. At the manger scene near the end, Sarah strokes the donkey — the animal that carried Mary, that will carry the Holy Family into Egypt as refugees. The nuns' Christmas card depicted The Flight into Egypt. Sarah, who has lost her own baby, strokes the donkey at the manger. Keegan doesn't underline it. She just puts it there.

◆ The Crows

"It was a December of crows." They roost in the huge old trees around the convent, walk the streets, find lookout posts, scavenge for what is dead. In Irish folk tradition, crows are associated with death and memory — they mark the site of what happened there. At the convent, they are the witnesses the human community refuses to be. They watch, they gather, they caw their disapproval. Keegan gives the crows the moral clarity no human in the novel will voice publicly. The novel ends but the crows remain.

◆ Keegan's Language — "and and and and"

Keegan's prose is short, declarative, precise. But her most revealing syntactic device is the polysyndeton — the repeated and that chains obligations together without pause: "to carry on, to keep his head down and stay on the right side of people, and to keep providing for his girls and see them getting on and completing their education at St Margaret's." One obligation leading inexorably to the next, no space to stop and think. It is the syntax of a man who cannot afford reflection. It is also the Biblical rhythm of the King James Bible — the sacred cadence applied to the relentless pressure of ordinary survival. The restraint of the language mirrors the restraint of the culture: a society that has learned not to speak, not to name, not to see.

◆ The Ending — "The Thing Not Done"

"You'll come home with me now, Sarah." Eight words. Not a speech, not a confrontation — just her name and the word home. The final paragraph reframes the entire moral calculus: "the worst that could have happened was also already behind him; the thing not done, which could have been — which he would have had to live with for the rest of his life." The greatest moral failure is not doing. The consequences ahead are bad, but less bad than leaving her there again. And in his "foolish heart" — the word is deliberate — he believes they will manage. Keegan won't let him be a hero even at the end. He is foolish, afraid, and he goes anyway.

◆ The Title — What It Means

In the final chapter, thinking of Mrs Wilson, Furlong arrives at the novel's thesis directly: "the small things she had said and done and had refused to do and say and what she must have known, the things which, when added up, amounted to a life." The title is not a general statement about kindness. It is Mrs Wilson's eulogy. And by extension, Ned's. The small things — a word of praise, a hot water bottle, a dictionary, polished shoes, a late conversation at the fire — are what constitute human dignity. The laundries were a system for the systematic destruction of exactly those things: a name, warmth, the knowledge that someone sees you. Bill's act is a small thing. The novella insists that small things are, in fact, everything.

Keegan's Own Words

For me, it isn't a book about the laundries. For me, it's a book about a coal man who doesn't know who his father is because his mother gave birth to him out of wedlock. And I think it's more to do with hope and courage... I didn't want to write a story of cruelty and incarceration.

— Claire Keegan, NPR interview

Keegan has also stated that she refused to condemn any of her characters — not the Mother Superior, not Eileen, not the townspeople who look away. This is a very specific authorial choice. The novel is not a morality play with villains. It is a moral test, applied to ordinary people in ordinary circumstances, and asking: what would you do?

The Film (2024)

Production

Directed by Tim Mielants (Belgian; known for Peaky Blinders). Screenplay by Enda Walsh (Irish playwright; Brooklyn, Hunger). Cillian Murphy produced it after pitching to Matt Damon on the set of Oppenheimer. Premiered at the Berlin Film Festival, February 2024. Budget: $3 million. Box office: $14.4 million. Filmed on location in New Ross.

Cast

Cillian Murphy as Bill Furlong. Emily Watson as the Mother Superior — chillingly cast, all smooth authority and implicit threat. Eileen Furlong played by Eileen Walsh — who starred in Peter Mullan's The Magdalene Sisters (2002). That casting is not accidental: she carries the weight of both films simultaneously.

Differences from the book

The film is remarkably faithful. Key cinematic additions: Bill's obsessive washing of coal-black hands becomes a recurring visual motif for complicity; Murphy renders interiority through breath, posture, and stillness rather than dialogue. The Mother Superior is given slightly more screen presence as a controlled, dangerous antagonist. The ending is slightly more open and abrupt than the book's final paragraph.

Critical reception

Widely praised for Murphy's restrained performance and for the film's fidelity to Keegan's prose rhythm — translating her minimalism into visual silence and negative space. Some critics noted that the interiority of the novel is inevitably reduced; others argued that Murphy externalises it through performance in a way that finds a genuine cinematic equivalent.

The Note on the Text

At the end of the book, Keegan does something unusual: she refuses to let the reader leave in the comfort of fiction. After the story ends, a factual note states that Ireland's last Magdalene laundry did not close until 1996; that ten thousand is the "modest" figure for women confined; that most records were "destroyed, lost, or made inaccessible"; and that the Mother and Baby Home Commission found that nine thousand children died in just eighteen investigated institutions.

The word "modest" is the author's voice making a quiet argument about undercounting. And the progression — "Many girls and women lost their babies. Some lost their lives. Some or most lost the lives they could have had" — culminates in that third category: not just dead, but denied the possibility of living. The small things, added up, that they never got to have. The note does what the novel alone cannot: it closes the distance between fiction and history. You leave the book holding both simultaneously.

Lara
Art History
The Magdalen Reading — Rogier van der Weyden (c.1435)
Mary Magdalene absorbed in reading, on the floor, eyes down, folded into herself. Behind her: a window she does not look at. At her feet: the small jar of ointment — her iconographic symbol. "In Catholic tradition the Magdalen was conflated with both Mary of Bethany... and the unnamed 'sinner' of Luke 7." Three women merged into one stereotype: the fallen woman who redeems herself through silence and suffering. The green of her dress. The closed posture. She is already inside.

Mitchell's mother was of Irish-Scottish descent. Key Academic and Journalistic Works

  • James M. Smith — Ireland's Magdalen Laundries and the Nation's Architecture of Containment (2007, Notre Dame University Press). The foundational academic text.
  • Frances Finnegan — Do Penance or Perish (2004). The first book-length history. Her dark observation: "Possibly the advent of the washing machine has been as instrumental in closing these laundries as have changing attitudes."
  • Mary Raftery & Eoin O'Sullivan — Suffer the Little Children (1999). Companion to the "States of Fear" documentary series.
  • Justice for Magdalenes Research — Oral History Project (2013–2015). ~3,000 pages of survivor testimonies. Archived at the Digital Repository of Ireland.
  • The McAleese Report — Report of the Inter-Departmental Committee to Establish the Facts of State Involvement with the Magdalen Laundries (2013).
  • Clara Fischer — "Gender, Nation, and the Politics of Shame," Signs 41(4) (2016).
  • Rebecca Lea McCarthy — Origins of the Magdalene Laundries (2010). Connects the laundries to post-colonial identity formation.
  • Maureen Sullivan — Girl in the Tunnel (2023). The most recent major survivor memoir. Entered the laundry at age 12.
  • Claire Keegan — Small Things Like These (2021, Faber). The definitive literary treatment.
  • Almudena Grandes — Las tres bodas de Manolita (2014). Addresses the Spanish Patronato system under Franco within her Civil War saga.

What Remains Unresolved

As of 2026, the religious orders have never issued an apology and have contributed nothing to the compensation scheme — despite receiving state funding for decades. Their archives remain closed to historians. Many women who died in the laundries lie in unmarked graves. The National Memorial at Seán McDermott Street is planned but not yet built. Justice for Magdalenes Research continues identifying the unidentified dead. The "woman in the home" language remains in the Irish Constitution after the failed 2024 referendum. The women who survived and never came forward are still out there — some elderly, some already gone. Their stories may never be collected.

Ireland Today — And the Shadow of the Past

Modern Ireland has changed almost beyond recognition from the country that sustained the Magdalene system. Same-sex marriage was legalised by referendum in 2015. The Eighth Amendment was repealed in 2018. The Mother and Baby Homes report was published in 2021. Yet the reckoning is still incomplete. The structural conditions that allowed the abuse — the delegation of state functions to unaccountable religious bodies, the culture of deference to authority, the stigmatisation of women outside normative categories — require not just apology but active institutional transformation.

Ireland's past is not past. It lives in the women who survived. It lives in the unmarked graves. It lives, faintly but insistently, in the steam from an ordinary washing machine.

Official Reports & Government Documents

Report of the Inter-Departmental Committee to Establish the Facts of State Involvement with the Magdalen Laundries (The McAleese Report)
Irish Government, February 2013. The central official inquiry into state involvement with the Magdalene laundries.
Commission to Inquire into Child Abuse Report (The Ryan Report)
Government of Ireland, May 2009. Documents abuse across industrial schools, reformatories and residential Church institutions.
Report of the Commission of Investigation into Mother and Baby Homes
Government of Ireland, January 2021. Documents the deaths of approximately 9,000 children across 18 institutions.
The Ferns Report
Government of Ireland, October 2005. Investigation into clerical sexual abuse in the Diocese of Ferns, County Wexford.
